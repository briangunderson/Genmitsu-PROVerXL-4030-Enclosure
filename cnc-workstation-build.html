Rolling Cart / Workbench

~$150–250 ▼

The foundation of the whole setup. Everything mounts to this, everything rolls with it. When you're done working, push it against the wall. When you want to cut, pull it out.

Requirements

  • Min surface size: 36" × 32" (enclosure needs ~35.4" × ~29.4" footprint)
  • Weight capacity: 300+ lbs (machine ~57 lbs + enclosure ~40 lbs + dust collection ~30 lbs + materials)
  • Locking casters: Non-negotiable. CNC vibration will walk an unlocked cart across the floor.
  • Lower shelf: For shop vac + cyclone separator

Recommended Options

  • Husky 46" Adjustable Height Workbench — steel frame, 46"×24" top, locking casters available, lower shelf. ~$200 at Home Depot.
  • Keter Folding Work Table — cheaper (~$90), lighter duty, but the 700 lb rating is sufficient. No lower shelf though.
  • DIY: 2×4 frame + 3/4" MDF top on heavy-duty casters — cheapest, fully customizable. Build a torsion box top for maximum flatness. Add a lower shelf from 3/4" plywood. Total materials ~$80–120.
Pro tip: Whatever you choose, add a 3/4" horse stall mat or thick rubber anti-fatigue mat between the cart and the floor. This decouples vibration from the finished basement floor and dramatically reduces transmitted noise to rooms above.

Enclosure (Aluminum Extrusion + Polycarbonate)

~$400–600 ▼

The enclosure does triple duty: dust containment, noise reduction, and laser safety. The frame is 30mm aluminum extrusion with polycarbonate panels. A hinged front door gives full access to the work area.

Design Reference

Jason Webb published a full open-source design with Fusion 360 files and parts lists for exactly this machine: github.com/jasonwebb/Genmitsu-PROVerXL-4030-Enclosure. The design includes a slide-out drawer so you can pull the CNC out of the enclosure for workpiece changes, which is a clever touch. Your fork: github.com/briangunderson/Genmitsu-PROVerXL-4030-Enclosure

Key Dimensions (from the reference design)

  • Frame outer: ~35.4" W × 29.4" D × 29.4" H
  • Extrusion: 30mm (EXM-3030) from T-Nutz
  • Panels: 0.220" (~6mm) thick sheets

Modifications for Your Build

  • Use polycarbonate instead of acrylic. You're running a 10W+ 450nm laser. Polycarbonate blocks UV and near-UV wavelengths far better than acrylic, and it won't shatter. Get OD4+ rated orange/amber tinted polycarb sheets for the door panel so you can watch the laser work safely.
  • Add a 4" dust port on the back panel — a simple bulkhead fitting or a 3D-printed flange.
  • Add a 4" fume exhaust port on a side or back panel for laser ventilation (separate from dust collection).
  • Add foam weatherstrip tape to all panel edges and the door seal for improved noise isolation.
Laser safety: A 10W 450nm diode laser can cause instant, permanent eye damage from even reflected/scattered light. Clear acrylic or polycarbonate is NOT sufficient. Use OD4+ rated 450nm laser safety panels (orange/amber tinted) for any panel you'll look through while the laser is running. Alternatively, use opaque panels on all sides except the door.

Dust Collection System

~$100–180 ▼

The DeWalt DWP611 on a PROVerXL cutting MDF or hardwood produces a LOT of fine dust. The enclosure contains it, but you need to actively pull it out. A shop vac with a cyclone separator is the standard approach — the cyclone catches 95%+ of the debris before it hits the vac filter, which means less filter changes and better sustained suction.

Setup

  • Cyclone separator (Dust Deputy or similar) mounts to a 5-gallon bucket on the lower shelf of the cart
  • Shop vac sits next to it on the same shelf
  • 4" flex hose runs from the enclosure's dust port down to the cyclone inlet
  • Cyclone outlet connects to shop vac inlet
  • Short run, no permanent plumbing — everything self-contained on the cart

Auto-Start

An iVac Pro or similar current-sensing switch plugs inline with the DeWalt's power. When the router spins up, the switch automatically turns on the shop vac. When you stop the router, the vac runs for a few more seconds (adjustable delay) to clear residual dust, then shuts off. Zero manual steps.

Smart home angle: Since you're a Home Assistant user, you could also automate this with a smart plug + power monitoring on the router circuit. But honestly, a dedicated iVac is more reliable for this — it's purpose-built, handles motor inrush current properly, and doesn't depend on your HA instance being up.

Laser Fume Ventilation

~$80–130 ▼

Laser cutting/engraving produces fumes that are genuinely hazardous to breathe — burning wood releases CO, VOCs, and fine particulates; acrylic releases cyanide compounds. You need active extraction to outside, and you've got a basement door and casement windows to work with.

Setup

  • Inline duct fan (4" AC Infinity Cloudline T4 or similar) mounted in-line between the enclosure exhaust port and the window
  • 4" insulated flex duct runs from enclosure to window — insulated duct reduces condensation and noise
  • Window insert — cut a piece of 1" rigid foam insulation board to fit a casement window opening. Drill a 4" hole, mount a dryer vent cap on the outside face. When done, remove the insert and close the window.
  • Optional: charcoal pre-filter inside the enclosure before the exhaust port, to reduce odor. Not a substitute for venting outside, but helps with residual smell.
Casement window note: Casement windows open outward with a crank. Your foam insert sits in the frame when the window is cranked open, held in place by friction fit or a couple of spring clips. Seal the edges with adhesive foam tape for a snug fit. Takes 10 seconds to install/remove.

Sound Dampening

~$30–60 ▼

The DeWalt DWP611 is rated around 79 dB by DeWalt, but in practice when cutting it's 90+ dB. The enclosure with sealed panels will knock that down 15–25 dB depending on how tight your seals are. Combined with the rubber mat under the cart, you're looking at "loud conversation" levels (~65–70 dB) outside the enclosure instead of "jet engine in a closet."

Key strategies (most are already covered above)

  • Enclosure panels: 1/4" polycarbonate provides good mass for sound blocking
  • Seal all gaps: Sound leaks through the smallest opening — use rubber gasket/weatherstrip on every panel edge and the door seal
  • Rubber mat under cart: Decouples vibration from the floor
  • Optional: mass-loaded vinyl (MLV) on the inside of opaque panels adds significant sound blocking. 1 lb/sqft MLV is the standard for sound isolation.
  • Optional: acoustic foam on the inside ceiling of the enclosure reduces resonance (not to be confused with sound blocking — foam absorbs reflections inside, MLV blocks transmission outside)
Reality check: Even with all this, running a DeWalt router in a basement will be audible upstairs. It won't be obnoxious, but it won't be silent. The laser, on the other hand, is nearly silent — just the fan noise.

"Always Ready" Control Setup

~$50–200 ▼

The goal: walk up, flip one switch (or zero switches), and start cutting. No hunting for laptops, no plugging in USB cables, no launching software. This is the ADHD-friendly CNC station.

Dedicated Controller

  • Option A: Cheap refurb laptop ($50–100) — a ThinkPad T450 or similar from eBay. Runs gSender, Candle, or LightBurn permanently. Lid closed, sitting on the cart shelf or mounted to the side with a VESA bracket. Connect a small monitor or use the laptop screen.
  • Option B: Raspberry Pi + touchscreen ($80–120) — runs CNCjs or a web-based sender. Compact, low-power, always-on. Pair with a 7" touchscreen for a clean setup.
  • Option C: Your offline controller — you already bought the Genmitsu offline controller. For simple jobs loaded from SD card, this works with zero computer needed. Not great for complex jobs or laser work though.

Permanent Wiring

  • USB panel mount passthrough — USB-B (or whatever your GRBL board uses) on the enclosure panel, USB-A on the outside to the controller. Cable stays connected to the CNC board permanently.
  • Monitor arm: clamp-style VESA mount attached to the cart frame or a shelf post. A 15–17" monitor at eye level. No wall mounting needed.
  • Power strip on the cart frame — one switch for everything. Router, controller, monitor, shop vac (via iVac), laser PSU, vent fan.

Software Suggestions

  • CNC routing: gSender (free, great UI, runs on everything)
  • Laser: LightBurn ($60, worth every penny for a 10W+ laser)
  • CAD/CAM: Fusion 360 (free personal), Carbide Create (free), or Vectric VCarve

Suggested Build Order

▼

Phase 1 — Get Cutting Fast (Weekend 1)

  • Set up the rolling cart/workbench with rubber mat
  • Mount the PROVerXL to the cart surface (bolt through MDF spoilboard)
  • Set up the dedicated controller (laptop + USB) and test connectivity
  • Run test cuts in open air just to verify everything works after the move

Phase 2 — Enclosure (Weekend 2–3)

  • Order aluminum extrusion and connectors from T-Nutz (ships in ~1 week)
  • Order polycarbonate panels — have them cut to size if possible
  • Assemble frame, mount panels, install door with hinges and latches
  • Install dust port and exhaust port bulkheads
  • Install USB passthrough

Phase 3 — Dust + Ventilation (same weekend as enclosure)

  • Set up cyclone separator + shop vac on lower shelf
  • Connect hoses, install iVac switch
  • Build the casement window foam insert with vent cap
  • Run exhaust duct from enclosure to window
  • Test: run the router for 5 minutes cutting MDF, check dust containment
  • Test: run the laser for 5 minutes, check fume extraction

Phase 4 — Polish (whenever)

  • Add MLV / acoustic foam if noise is still a problem
  • Mount monitor arm, clean up cable routing
  • Add LED strip lighting inside the enclosure (makes a huge difference for visibility)
  • Optional: add a cheap webcam inside the enclosure for remote monitoring via your phone
